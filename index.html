WaterNet - A Network for Monitoring and Assessing Water Quality

Time Period: Mar 2024 - May 2024


During my project on WaterNet, I spearheaded the development of an advanced network for real-time water quality monitoring. This initiative involved creating a system to assess water suitability for drinking and irrigation using Machine Learning (ML) tools.

I designed a network architecture utilizing LoRa technology to collect data on water parameters, considering land topology for optimal performance. To address the lack of large, open datasets, I developed new datasets specifically for training ML models. I applied three ML models—Random Forest (RF), Logistic Regression (LR), and Support Vector Machine (SVM)—to classify water quality.

The results indicated that LR was most effective for drinking water, while SVM performed better for irrigation water. Through meticulous experimentation and refinement, I optimized the models to achieve high classification accuracy. This project demonstrates my proficiency in integrating network technology with ML tools to solve complex water quality assessment challenges. The WaterNet system has significant potential for real-time water monitoring and improving water management practices.

Statistical Models for Early Detection of Cardiac Arrest in Newborn Babies

Time Period: - Present


Currently, I am working on a project focused on the early detection of cardiac arrest in newborns using statistical models. This project addresses a critical challenge in neonatal care, where timely detection is crucial for improving survival rates and long-term health outcomes.

The research involves developing a Cardiac Machine Learning Model (CMLM) that utilizes statistical techniques such as logistic regression and support vector machines (SVM) to identify early warning signs of cardiac arrest. The model is being designed to analyze a comprehensive dataset of physiological parameters collected from newborns in the Cardiac Intensive Care Unit (CICU).

By focusing on subtle changes in heart rate, respiratory rate, and oxygen saturation, the model aims to predict the likelihood of cardiac arrest with high accuracy.

Future work includes validating the model with historical data and conducting real-time testing in a clinical setting. The goal is to refine the model's parameters and integrate it into existing CICU monitoring systems, ensuring effective operation alongside other medical tools. This project is intended to establish a new standard for early detection of cardiac arrest in newborns, ultimately enhancing patient outcomes and reducing the incidence of critical events in neonatal care.
